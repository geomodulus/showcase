The new TTC Line 1 Subway extension is taking another big step forward with the release of a tunnelling RFQ. But what is this project, why are we building it, and when can we get on board?

What we know so far:

The Yonge North subway extension is a planned 8-kilometre extension of Toronto's Line 1, more specifically its Yonge side from the longtime terminus at Finch station, north along Yonge St., to just beyond Highway 7 at Richmond Hill Centre in York Region.

The project is designed in such a way that the subway will begin continuing in newly excavated tunnel tubes north into York Region. After passing Royal Orchard Blvd, the tunnels will swing east underground, and then back to the north to pop above ground next to the GO Richmond Hill Line (discussed in a previous article about the “Don Line”). The subway will then continue a short distance before terminating at a station called “High Tech”, named after the adjacent street at Richmond Hill Centre.

Between Finch and High Tech will be four or five additional stations, at Steeles Ave., Clark Ave., Royal Orchard, and “Bridge”; there's also a possible station at Cummer Ave.. The stations at Steeles, Clark, and Royal Orchard should be fairly standard subway stations; however, as the line will pop above ground at the north end, both Bridge and High Tech will be surface stations.

It should be noted that the smart approach for Cummer station is to provision for building it in the future, even if not as part of this project. North York Centre station was added after the extension to Finch had already been completed as development happened around it and something similar could happen at Cummer - which is already seeing the massive M2N development.

Bridge station is so named because it will pass under both Highways 7 and 407, and bridge the areas on the north and the south. This location is actually quite convenient from a transit connection perspective, as Viva BRT buses travelling east and west on Highway 7 and GO regional buses travelling east and west on Highway 407 will all be able to connect with the subway here. There will also be a connection to Langstaff GO on the existing Richmond Hill line, which will hopefully be renamed in line with the new subway station.

Interestingly, High Tech station will only be a few hundred metres north of Bridge, creating possibly the smallest gap between subway stations on the network (the current shortest is between Osgoode and St. Andrew). To the north of High Tech will be a small subway yard, which previous plans had underground. This yard will be used for storing some trains for use at the beginning of service, as well as light maintenance work.

Probably the most visible urban change that will arise from the project (especially since the trains themselves will mostly be underground) are the two massive planned transit-oriented communities (TOCs) that will be placed around Richmond Hill Centre, to the north and south of Highways 7 and 407. These developments would include tens of towers, putting thousands of new residents within a short walk of the new subway, although they have been controversial due to their truly extreme density. That being said, this level of vertical density is probably appropriate given the high station density being proposed for the area!

The projections for the project call for an eventual daily ridership of over 100,000, which is high but perhaps not so surprising given the legions of buses which run up and down Yonge St. north of Finch station. Taking this significant bus traffic off of the streets and removing the dog leg at the end of many east-west TTC routes in the area will be a big benefit of the project. That being said, if ridership recovery moves at a decent pace, it will be critical that the Ontario Line is completed and operational before this project, as extending the Yonge Subway northwards will only add to its legendary congestion - which the Ontario Line is designed to tackle.

What was announced:

Last week, various levels of government and transit authorities came together to announce the RFQ for early tunnel works on the project, which is exciting but perhaps a bit less so than some might expect.

Metrolinx, in what appears to be a move mostly driven by the delays and high costs of projects like the Eglinton Crosstown, has been breaking its new subway projects into several smaller contracts rather than a single “mega” contract. The idea here is that by creating several smaller projects, more companies can throw their hat into the ring, creating more competition and a lower price for taxpayers, but this does mean large subway projects will now need to be brought together from several smaller components. It's also not entirely clear that this strategy is helping Toronto to shake its title as one of the most expensive places in the world to build rapid transit. (https://transitcosts.com/)

One of those smaller components is building tunnels - or “advanced tunnel works” - for each of the other two subway extension projects Metrolinx is currently undertaking (Scarborough Subway Extension & Eglinton West Subway Extension), which a separate consortium (a group of companies) has already been hired on and begun tunnelling. Separate companies will then be awarded contracts to fit those tunnels out with rails and other equipment as well as build stations.

In a procurement process, there are typically a number of steps, and the RFQ step is not exactly the most consequential. RFQ stands for “Request for Qualifications”, and in this step companies apply to be able to bid on the project - in this case the advanced tunnel works. It isn’t until the next step, known as RFP or “Request for Proposals”, that companies actually bid and a preferred construction team is chosen. Nonetheless, progress through this process absolutely does matter - and it seems likely that a final contract for tunnels could be signed in the next year or two.

Costs and Dates:

The Yonge subway extension is meant to be complete in the early 2030s, a few years after the Scarborough and Eglinton subway extensions, which makes sense given the delay in beginning tunnelling works and the desire for the much more complicated (and at risk of delay) Ontario Line to be completed first. Initial costing for the project is between 4.5 and 5.8B CAD, but given inflation, supply chain challenges, and the complexity of a major transit project as well as the risk of delays, that price could easily rise higher.
