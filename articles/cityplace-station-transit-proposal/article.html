Toronto has grown a lot in the last two decades, and everyone knows its transit systems have bowed under the pressure of that growth — from an increasingly fault-prone subway to streetcars that seemingly come when they feel like it.

One of the most visible projects created to improve things has been the much-delayed renovation of Union Station, which is now in its final stages. However, mention of Union raises a question: Should a city of Toronto’s size have to rely on a single city-centre station for all of its needs, especially as transformational GO-train service investments are being made to better connect the city with its suburbs?

I for one don’t think so, and so I’m proposing a new city-centre “shoulder” station named “CityPlace” after the nearby neighbourhood that embodies our city’s condo-driven transformation.

Read on for why and how I think this station should be built.

Why Toronto should build CityPlace station

A walk down King or Front St. W. from Union Station is a humbling experience as new glass and concrete towers have risen on seemingly every block. Over 70 per cent of residences in Ward 10 Spadina-Fort York are in high-rise apartment towers. But, despite this, high-density transportation is all but absent.

There are several streetcar routes in the area, such as those on King and Spadina that are among the most heavily-used surface transit routes in North America, but their speed and reliability is a perennial problem.

The coming Ontario Line will bring more subway service to the city centre, but the line frustratingly skirts dense areas like King-Spadina and CityPlace.

In the meantime, massive new developments have only added to the pressure. The Well alone has over 1,500 badly needed housing units, and they’re placed on top of a shopping centre that feels like a 21st century version of the Eaton Centre.

In addition to all of the residences are new offices, both at The Well and other west-end complexes that form regional destinations, which will only become more important as employers seeking hard-to-find talent centralise their footprints in Canada’s most accessible location — downtown Toronto.

And yet, increasingly, with downtown creeping west, that accessibility can be called into question. GO train riders working at The Well, or even attending events at the SkyDome (as it’s still known in our hearts), need to walk 15–20 minutes west from Union Station through crowded passages and on narrow sidewalks to their destinations.

And when problems arise at Union, the entire region becomes paralyzed. Local transit can’t pick up the slack given the sheer capacity differential between 300-metre-long double-decker GO trains and narrow streetcars just one-tenth the length.

What’s even more frustrating is that if one peers over the edge of the bridge crossing the railway tracks at Spadina Ave., they are greeted with a seemingly unending flow of trains headed to all sorts of destinations — including Pearson Airport, Mississauga, Barrie, and Hamilton.

Conversely, a rider heading downtown from one of those spots can look up in frustration as the train streams past their actual destination.

Union Station is already a perpetually busy site, and that’s only set to increase as train service dramatically ramps up to levels never before seen, new massive office complexes rise on all sides, and the entire Quebec City–Windsor corridor continues to grow.

It needs a pressure-relief valve, and that’s where CityPlace station comes in.

How Toronto should build CityPlace station

CityPlace station would be a new regional train station on the west end of downtown Toronto — just south of Front and Spadina, spanning the array of railway tracks. It would be truly revolutionary for access in this transit-starved part of town, and it would make use of a truly rare site on the GO network — one where trains from all lines run just metres apart.

A new station here would appear as a large open extension of the sidewalk along Spadina, which could be accessed in a way that contrasts sharply with the labyrinthine underground corridors of Union Station.

Unlike at Union, passengers wouldn’t need to wait for the mad rush to a platform announced on an overhead screen minutes before their train departure. Instead, they could walk in, pass through a fare gate, and descend directly to one of three wide platforms serving trains on the Barrie, Kitchener, and Lakeshore West corridors.

These platforms would be seeing a lot of trains, as 10-minute-or-better service is expected on all of these lines as part of the GO expansion program.

Much of the space that would be used to create CityPlace station is currently taken up by a rail yard and a rail underpass. Fortunately, changes to the way train service is operated on the transformed GO network will make parking trains in the yard midday a much less frequent occurrence, while a reconsidered routing into Union Station makes the underpass unnecessary — thus freeing the space for a station.

While unique in Canada, CityPlace station would be only the latest in a long line of similar stations around the world that accommodate additional journeys to and from areas adjacent to city centres. In many places — particularly in Asia and Europe — it would be unheard of to not have a railway station serving an area as dense as CityPlace.

Could it really happen?

Of course, the ultimate question remains: Could such a station really be built? How much would it cost?

Well, at the moment, plans are actually moving forward for a Spadina-Front GO station in partnership with a private developer who intends to build it along with a Front St. W. tower across from The Well. Unfortunately, though, this station will only serve trains on the Barrie GO line — not the Lakeshore West, which is the busiest line in the system.

Expanding this plan wouldn’t be inexpensive. Based on the cost of the remaining SmartTrack stations, we’d be looking at a likely cost in the hundreds of millions of dollars.

This is a high price to pay, but when you consider how many passengers it could serve — particularly during events at the SkyDome — its costs seem less extreme.

Also, as more and more high rises go up nearby, the site will only become more expensive to work on the longer we wait. While a station could be built fairly quickly on the site as it stands today, the plans for condo development and a potential new rail deck district would seriously complicate things should they not consider provisioning for a station.

Long story short: This plan could increase transit accessibility in a rapidly growing part of the city, and take a huge load off the shoulders of Union Station. But if it’s going to happen, it needs to happen sooner rather than later — or else the odds will get stacked too high against it.
